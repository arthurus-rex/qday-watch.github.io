The Q-Day Watch Compendium

Quantum computers are getting bigger by the day, threatening to break classical asymmetric cryptography. As with any digital threat, speculation and misinformation abound. Concision and clarity are the solution. This website contains my personal notes on the most recent academic and industry developments in quantum computing and post-quantum cryptographic algorithms.

Timelines

At this time, the largest quantum computers (17,000 qubits) have a sufficient number of qubits to break classical encryption with the newer, theoretical improved Shor's algorithm published by Oratomic (still being confirmed). However, the neutral atom quantum computers (such as those made by CalTech and ETH Zürich) have not yet been demonstrated to perform all quantum operations necessary to implement Shor's. Therefore, Q-day has not been confirmed, although clandestine quantum computers with these capabilities may exist for some time before their public announcement.

[April 20th, 2026] Quantum computers are not a threat to 128-bit symmetric keys

Filippo Valsorda - Blog by a senior cryptographic engineer and software developer on the Golang team.

Good breakdown on why Grover's algorithm has not been shown to provide any threatening speedup to brute-forcing AES-128. Valsorda's opinion here reflects the majority opinion of cryptographic experts at this time, including NIST and Germany's BSI. CNSA 2.0 (NSA) is the primary dissent, requiring 256-bit security for all algorithms including AES and SHA as a method of future-proofing. However, AES-256 incurrs a hefty performance detriment and increases overall migration effort, with Valsorda saying, "Conflating necessary and unnecessary changes will cause needless churn and take resources away from the urgent updates."

[April 8th, 2026] A new trick brings stability to quantum computers

ETH Zürich - Oliver Morsch, freelance author

This is a press release about an academic development at ETH Zurich, where researchers improved upon neutral atom quantum computing by creating a SWAP gate, also called quantum exchange, with only geometric phase. Geometric phase causes the particle state to switch depending on its path, and is quite robust against noise because neutral particles stay in a low-energy state, leveraging tunneling and collisions to apply these swap gates to thousands of qubits simultaneously.

Geometric phase is a cool trick involving changing the electron spin of a qubit by 360 degrees so that it points in the same direction, but this changes the phase of the wavefunction by 180 degrees, effectively flipping it. Then, the researchers brought the qubits close enough in space so the wavefunctions overlapped in superposition. Geometric phase is independent of how quickly the atoms are manipulated, making it highly precise (99.91%). Entanglement could be created with half-SWAP gates, induced by atomic collisions, but this has not yet been done by the researchers.

[April 6th, 2026] A cryptography engineer's perspective on quantum computing timelines

Filippo Valsorda - Blog by a senior cryptographic engineer and software developer on the Golang team

Valsorda describes the reasoning behind his change of position regarding PQC and why the transition to PQC must be accelerated, and announces ML-DSA will be added to X.509 with exception for Merkle Tree Certifikates for WebPKI. His advice is "any non-PQ key exchange should be considered a potential active compromise, worthy of warning" and "we need to forget about non-interactive key exchanges (NIKEs) for a while." He also advocates for not bothering with hybrid classic+PQC crypto, and suggests that symmetric encryption should be at 128-bit strength (as opposed to the 256-bit strength that CNSA 2.0 demands). And, he draws attention to file encryption being vulnerable and the need to "error out on non-PQC age recipient types soon."
