The Q-Day Watch Compendium

Quantum computers are getting bigger by the day, threatening to break classical asymmetric cryptography. As with any digital threat, speculation and misinformation abound. Concision and clarity are the solution. This website contains my personal notes on the most recent academic and industry developments in quantum computing and post-quantum cryptographic algorithms.

Timelines

At this time, the largest quantum computers (17,000 qubits) have a sufficient number of qubits to break classical encryption with the newer, theoretical improved Shor's algorithm published by Oratomic (still being confirmed). However, the neutral atom quantum computers (such as those made by CalTech and ETH Zürich) have not yet been demonstrated to perform all quantum operations necessary to implement Shor's. Therefore, Q-day has not been confirmed, although clandestine quantum computers with these capabilities may exist for some time before their public announcement.

[April 26th, 2026] The predictable failure of the QDay Prize

Craig Gidney

In response to the awarding of the QDay Prize, Craig Gidney, a software engineer on Google's quantum computing team, published a response on a personal, independent blog. In this article, Gidney describes having been approached last year by Project Eleven coordinators to solicit a submission to the QDay Prize. Gidney declined this request and warned the organizers that they would likely receive submissions that implement Shor's algorithm on a small scale and do not account for error correction, thus performing on a quantum computer no better than on a random number generator, as described in Gidney's delightful April Fool's Day submission to the Association for Computational Heresy, Falling with Style: Factoring up to 255 "with" a Quantum Computer. Now that the QDay prize has been awarded, Gidney claims that the winner used a "Falling with Style" trick, because even though Lelli seems to have constructed a valid quantum circuit, it still performs no better than a random number generator would in the place of a quantum computer, a problem which Project Eleven has acknowledged. Gidney's closing remarks are most poignant:

"There are legitimate concerns that quantum computers could become cryptographically relevant before the end of the decade. This is why companies like Google and CloudFlare are accelerating their post-quantum cryptography transitions. The ostensible goal of the QDay Prize was to raise awareness about this. Frustratingly, it has likely achieved the opposite result. It will no doubt be quipped alongside other gotcha-style arguments, like “call me back after you’ve factored 21”.

[April 24th, 2026] 15-Bit ECC Key Broken on Quantum Hardware Wins Q-Day Prize

The Quantum Insider - Mohib Ur Rehman

Independent researcher Giancarlo Lelli derived the private key from a 15-bit elliptic curve public key using standard cloud-available cloud computing hardware (IBM Quantum and Qiskit), then published his code and strategy to GitHub. For these efforts, Lelli won The QDay Prize from Project Eleven, a one-Bitcoin award (valued at ~$77,800 at time of writing) for breaking the biggest ECC key with Shor's algorithm. 15-bit encryption is far from the 256-bit encryption protecting the Internet at large.

ADDENDUM: April 28th, 2026 - Craig Gidney, the author of a truly excellent work of academic comedy has weighed in on this topic. Gidney's April Fool's paper describes a genuine, common fallacy associated with Shor's algorithm: when factoring small numbers, the performance of Shor's algorithm when using a quantum computer is no different than using a random number generator. Lelli's QDay Prize contribution, according to Gidney, does not pass the sniff test. The Quantum Insider, as of the writing of this addendum, has not updated their article.

[April 20th, 2026] Quantum computers are not a threat to 128-bit symmetric keys

Filippo Valsorda - Blog by a senior cryptographic engineer and software developer on the Golang team.

Good breakdown on why Grover's algorithm has not been shown to provide any threatening speedup to brute-forcing AES-128. Valsorda's opinion here reflects the majority opinion of cryptographic experts at this time, including NIST and Germany's BSI. CNSA 2.0 (NSA) is the primary dissent, requiring 256-bit security for all algorithms including AES and SHA as a method of future-proofing. However, AES-256 incurrs a hefty performance detriment and increases overall migration effort, with Valsorda saying, "Conflating necessary and unnecessary changes will cause needless churn and take resources away from the urgent updates."

[April 8th, 2026] A new trick brings stability to quantum computers

ETH Zürich - Oliver Morsch, freelance author

This is a press release about an academic development at ETH Zurich, where researchers improved upon neutral atom quantum computing by creating a SWAP gate, also called quantum exchange, with only geometric phase. Geometric phase causes the particle state to switch depending on its path, and is quite robust against noise because neutral particles stay in a low-energy state, leveraging tunneling and collisions to apply these swap gates to thousands of qubits simultaneously.

Geometric phase is a cool trick involving changing the electron spin of a qubit by 360 degrees so that it points in the same direction, but this changes the phase of the wavefunction by 180 degrees, effectively flipping it. Then, the researchers brought the qubits close enough in space so the wavefunctions overlapped in superposition. Geometric phase is independent of how quickly the atoms are manipulated, making it highly precise (99.91%). Entanglement could be created with half-SWAP gates, induced by atomic collisions, but this has not yet been done by the researchers.

[April 6th, 2026] A cryptography engineer's perspective on quantum computing timelines

Filippo Valsorda - Blog by a senior cryptographic engineer and software developer on the Golang team

His advice is "any non-PQ key exchange should be considered a potential active compromise, worthy of warning" and "we need to forget about non-interactive key exchanges (NIKEs) for a while." He also advocates for not bothering with hybrid classic+PQC crypto, and suggests that symmetric encryption should be at 128-bit strength (as opposed to the 256-bit strength that CNSA 2.0 demands). And, he draws attention to file encryption being vulnerable and the need to "error out on non-PQC age recipient types soon."

[August 27th, 2024] Quantum error correction below the surface code threshold

Google Quantum AI and Collaborators - (100+ collaborators listed on Page 8 from Google Research and DeepMind, University of Massachusetts Amherst, UC Santa Barbara, University of Connecticut Storrs, Auburn University, ETH Zurich in Switzerland, MIT, UC Riverside, and Yale)

Seminal research paper published in the journal Nature as open access. Quantum error correction (combining many physical qubits into one logical qubit) is critical for Shor's algorithm, and requires a physical error rate to be below a critical threshold to be feasible for real-world execution. The authors empirically demonstrate that Google's superconducting processor, Willow, is capable of a sufficient degree of quantum error correction to fulfill the requirements for a cryptographically-relevant quantum computer should it be scaled-up successfully. Experiments were run on 74-qubit and 105-qubit processors.

Addendum: April 29th, 2026 - At time of writing, the Willow quantum computer scaled to 105 qubits in July 2025, but it has not scaled further since, which has been the case across-the-board for superconducting quantum computers. Since then, Google and others have achieved better scaling results with neutral atom quantum computers.
